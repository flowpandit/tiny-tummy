## Design Context

### Users
Tiny Tummy is designed first for anxious new parents. They are often sleep-deprived, checking the app in short, high-stress moments while holding a baby, and trying to quickly answer a simple but emotionally loaded question: is this normal?

The product should reduce friction for fast logging while also reducing uncertainty. The core jobs to be done are:
- reassure parents when a pattern is normal
- help them log bowel and related care data quickly
- make it easy to notice when something actually deserves attention

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel calm, reassuring, and informative. It should never feel clinical in a cold way, and it should never feel playful at the expense of trust.

The emotional goal is to lower anxiety through clarity. The interface should communicate steadiness, warmth, and confidence without becoming decorative or sentimental.

### Aesthetic Direction
Keep the current visual direction as the foundation: warm, intentional, soft, and editorial rather than generic app-minimal. Improvements should be purposeful, not novelty-driven.

The product should support both light and dark themes, with equal care given to legibility and emotional tone in each. Design decisions should prioritize ease of use, especially in tired, distracted, one-handed contexts.

Anti-direction:
- anything that reads as AI-generated UI
- decorative trends without a functional reason
- generic startup polish that weakens trust or clarity

### Design Principles
- Reassure before you impress. The interface should reduce worry and answer “is this normal?” quickly.
- Optimize for tired parents. Fast scanning, low-friction logging, and forgiving interactions matter more than novelty.
- Keep warmth, remove noise. Preserve the current soft, calm direction, but only add visual complexity when it supports comprehension or trust.
- Make purpose visible. Every motion, accent, illustration, and layout choice should help orientation, clarity, or emotional grounding.
- Avoid AI slop. No generic gradients, templated card patterns, empty decoration, or trend-driven styling that makes the product feel interchangeable.
